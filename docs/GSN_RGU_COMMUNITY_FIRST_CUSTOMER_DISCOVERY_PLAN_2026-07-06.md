## 1. Decision

The next customer discovery event should start with the community, not with
Amara.

Amara can still appear as a short member example, but she should no longer carry
the whole session. The room should first recognise its own trust system, its own
memory, and its own gaps. Only then should one member story be used to test
whether that community evidence can travel responsibly.

The upgraded customer discovery story should begin with the community:

**The Nigerian Society at RGU already creates trust, memory, introductions,
support, accountability, and opportunity. GSN is being tested as infrastructure
that can help that community preserve, organise, communicate, and mobilise that
value.**

The discovery question is therefore:

**Can a real community see itself in GSN as trust infrastructure?**

Not:

**Can one fictional individual become believable?**

### What the workshop must prove

By the end of the session, the team should know whether participants can:

- name real trust-producing activities inside the society;
- explain which evidence would help decisions and which evidence would be
  dangerous;
- identify who should be allowed to confirm different kinds of evidence;
- distinguish evidence from a guarantee;
- say whether GSN feels like community infrastructure or just another personal
  profile;
- choose which story is strongest enough for the next real-world pilot.

---

## 2. Unabated Truth

The Amara story was useful when the product needed a simple portable-trust
example. It made an abstract idea human.

It is now too narrow for this stage.

Why:

1. It makes the session about one person's credibility rather than the
   community that creates credibility.
2. It can accidentally make GSN sound like a personal reputation app.
3. It hides the institutional buyer/user question: which real communities need
   this infrastructure enough to organise around it?
4. It lets participants react to a story instead of revealing how their own
   society already creates, loses, verifies, and transfers trust.
5. It risks polite feedback because participants may judge whether Amara is a
   good story rather than whether GSN solves a real community problem.

Devil's advocate: a moving personal story can make GSN feel emotionally clear,
but it can also hide the harder question: **who would adopt this, govern it,
pay attention to it, and trust it when real people disagree?**

The better route is community-first discovery, then individual member stories as
downstream proof.

---

## 3. Research Basis

This structure is more defensible than the Amara-first version because it tests
the riskiest assumptions in the room, not in the founder's imagination.

### Customer discovery discipline

Customer discovery should test assumptions outside the founder's head. For GSN,
the riskiest assumption is not:

**Can we tell a moving personal story?**

The riskiest assumption is:

**Will real communities recognise this as infrastructure for value they already
create?**

The workshop must turn GSN's community-first hypothesis into observable
participant evidence.

### Community-based research

Community-based research argues for involving community members and
organisational representatives in the research process, using their expertise to
understand the problem and produce knowledge that benefits the community.

For GSN, this means participants should not merely watch a demo. They should map
how trust already works inside their society, what records are missing, what
should stay private, and what evidence would make community support safer.

### Communities of practice

Research on communities of practice warns that real work often differs from
formal descriptions, manuals, and organisation charts.

For GSN, this means the event should ask how the Nigerian Society actually
supports members, introduces people, resolves doubts, helps newcomers, and
remembers contribution. A formal officer list is useful, but it is not the whole
truth.

### Information asymmetry

GSN's market problem is information asymmetry: one group may know that a person,
merchant, member, or organiser is reliable, while outsiders cannot see the same
evidence.

The discovery session must test whether structured community evidence can
reduce uncertainty without pretending to guarantee trust.

### Commons and governance

Community trust behaves like a shared resource. It depends on boundaries,
membership, local rules, monitoring, conflict resolution, and layered authority.

For GSN, the workshop must ask whether the community can define:

- who belongs;
- who can confirm what;
- which evidence counts;
- what is private;
- what can be shared outside the community;
- how disputes or corrections should be handled.

## 4. New Core Story

Use this story throughout the event:

**The Nigerian Society at RGU is more than a student group. It is a trust
community. New students need orientation, introductions, support, housing tips,
job leads, merchant recommendations, contribution groups, welfare help, and safe
referrals. Today, much of that trust lives in people's memory, WhatsApp threads,
officer knowledge, and personal introductions. When leaders change, members
graduate, or students move city, some of that value can disappear. GSN is being
tested as a way for the society to preserve community memory, record useful
trust evidence, and help members carry recognised value into support, trade,
referrals, and opportunity without turning trust into a fake score or automatic
guarantee.**

Short version:

**Can GSN help the Nigerian Society preserve and communicate the trust it
already creates?**

Plain spoken version:

**Your society already helps people know who belongs, who contributes, who shows
up, and who can be introduced safely. GSN is a test of whether that knowledge can
be preserved carefully, shared only where appropriate, and used to support better
decisions.**

## 5. Where Amara Fits Now

Amara becomes a member case study, not the main discovery frame.

Use her only after the community has been established:

**Imagine a member like Amara who has been active, reliable, supportive, and
known in one community. If she later needs support, trade, referral, or an
introduction in another setting, what evidence should her community be able to
show, and what should remain private?**

Amara's role:

- She makes the community problem human.
- She helps participants reason about a concrete member.
- She tests portability after the society's trust system has been discussed.

Amara must not:

- replace the community as the customer discovery object;
- become proof that GSN works;
- make the event feel like a fictional sales pitch;
- imply that GSN personally declares her trustworthy.

Facilitator line:

**Amara is not the product. She is one way to test whether community evidence can
help a real person without exposing too much or promising too much.**

## 6. What The Added White Paper And Handbook Change

The added institutional documents strengthen the meeting in three practical
ways.

First, they make the discovery session a test of **behavioural continuity**, not
only portable trust. The question becomes:

**Do communities already recognise useful behaviour, and do they lose that
recognition when people move, leaders change, or records remain informal?**

Second, they introduce the idea of **behavioural capital**. This is internal
research language, not participant language. In the room, say:

**People build value through reliable behaviour over time. Communities often
know this, but the knowledge is not always preserved or easy to share.**

Third, they add a stricter traceability test:

**Every GSN feature shown in customer discovery must preserve a real human or
community process that already exists.**

If a demo screen cannot answer "which real community process does this
preserve?", do not show it in the meeting.

This matters because GSN becomes weaker when it looks like a pile of features.
It becomes stronger when every screen connects back to a recognisable community
job: welcoming, confirming, supporting, referring, remembering, correcting, or
protecting.

### Language rule for the meeting

Use simple participant language:

- "community memory" instead of "behavioural continuity";
- "reliable behaviour over time" instead of "behavioural placement";
- "value people have already earned" instead of "behavioural capital";
- "evidence that helps a decision" instead of "institutional trust
  instrument".

Keep the formal terms for the university/research brief, not for the first
student discussion.

Do not ask students to admire the theory. Ask them to tell you where the theory
is wrong, incomplete, or useful.

## 7. Storyline Library For Discovery

Use one anchor story and no more than two contrast stories in a single meeting.

Devil's advocate: using every story at once will make GSN look unfocused. The
storyline library exists so the facilitator can choose the right example for the
room, not so every example becomes a mini-pitch.

### Anchor story: Nigerian Society at RGU

Use this as the main story for the planned meeting.

**The Nigerian Society helps students settle, find trustworthy information,
meet people, discover opportunities, and receive support. Much of this work
depends on memory, officer knowledge, WhatsApp, and personal introductions. GSN
tests whether the society can preserve useful community evidence without
turning human judgement into a score.**

Discovery test:

- What does the society already know about members that is useful but informal?
- What evidence should leaders be allowed to confirm?
- What should stay private?
- Which decisions would become safer with better community memory?
- What would make members refuse this, even if leaders liked it?

### Charity story: Pillar of Hope in Aberdeen

Use this if the room understands charity, welfare, volunteering, or community
service.

**Pillar of Hope supports people through welfare activity, volunteering,
referrals, donations, events, and practical help. The charity needs to know who
serves consistently, who can coordinate support, which volunteers are reliable,
which beneficiaries have received help, and which partners can be trusted.
GSN would not replace charity judgement. It would preserve service evidence,
volunteer history, support records, accountability, and community memory.**

Where GSN helps:

- volunteer participation and reliability records;
- welfare support history with privacy boundaries;
- coordinator responsibility and delegated authority;
- evidence for funders, partners, and trustees;
- continuity when charity leaders or volunteers change.

Discovery questions:

- What charity activities create trust?
- Who should confirm volunteer service?
- What beneficiary information must never be public?
- What evidence would help trustees, funders, or partners?
- How can the system avoid turning vulnerable people into public records?
- Where would human review still be required?

### Market story: Onitsha Main Market

Use this if the conversation turns to commerce, traders, credit, supplier
relationships, or market associations.

**Onitsha Main Market already operates as a trust network. Traders know who
honours commitments, who pays after receiving goods, who resolves disputes, who
keeps promises, and who has standing in a line or association. GSN would help
the market preserve commercial behavioural evidence and make selected trust
evidence usable for trade, referrals, merchant verification, and supplier
decisions.**

Where GSN helps:

- trader membership and line/association records;
- merchant verification;
- release-of-goods decision support;
- dispute and correction history;
- supplier confidence when dealing across distance.

Discovery questions:

- What makes a trader trusted in the market?
- Who is authorised to confirm a trader's standing?
- What evidence would a supplier need before releasing goods?
- What should a public TrustSlip show or refuse to show?
- How should disputes and false claims be corrected?
- What would make traders fear or reject this?

### Resident-to-merchant story: Aberdeen buyer and unknown member

Use this as the most personal, practical example.

**A resident in Aberdeen wants to trade with a GSN member or merchant they do
not personally know. The resident does not need the person's full private
history. They need enough current evidence to decide whether to proceed, reduce
the risk, ask for more confirmation, or walk away.**

Where GSN helps:

- public verification that the merchant or member exists in a real community;
- current TrustSlip status and expiry;
- merchant activity and confirmation boundaries;
- clear warnings about what GSN does not guarantee;
- a next action such as request community confirmation, proceed carefully, or
  ask for more evidence.

Discovery questions:

- What would you need before buying, referring, or releasing goods?
- Which evidence would make you more comfortable?
- Which evidence would still be insufficient?
- At what risk level would you require direct human confirmation?
- What should the page never imply?
- What would make you trust the page less?

### Church story: Redeemed Christian Church or an Aberdeen church

Use this if the audience recognises churches as organised trust communities.

**A church such as Redeemed Christian Church, or a local Aberdeen church, already
coordinates worship, departments, welfare, service teams, leadership, events,
member support, and community accountability. GSN would help the church preserve
service history, membership context, leadership responsibility, welfare
coordination, and trusted introductions while keeping pastoral judgement human
and private matters protected.**

Where GSN helps:

- department and branch organisation;
- service, volunteering, and leadership records;
- welfare coordination with privacy controls;
- member introductions and trusted referrals;
- continuity when pastors, department heads, or coordinators change.

Discovery questions:

- What church activities create trust?
- Who should confirm service, leadership, or membership?
- What should be visible to ordinary members, leaders, and outsiders?
- How can GSN support welfare without exposing sensitive pastoral information?
- What would make church leaders adopt or reject it?
- What would make ordinary members uncomfortable?

### Diaspora association story: UK-based Nigerian or ethnic community

Use this if participants discuss Abia, Igbo, Nigerian, student, town-union, or
UK diaspora coordination.

**A UK-based diaspora association helps members settle, organise meetings,
support one another, coordinate events, raise funds, introduce trusted people,
and preserve identity across distance. GSN would help the association keep
membership, contribution, service, leadership, welfare, and event evidence
organised so the community becomes stronger and more viable for society.**

Where GSN helps:

- membership and branch records;
- event participation and contribution history;
- welfare and emergency-support coordination;
- trusted introductions for housing, jobs, trade, and services;
- institutional memory across leadership changes.

Discovery questions:

- What community work is currently hidden in private chats and memory?
- Which records would make the association stronger?
- Who should approve membership, welfare support, or public confirmation?
- What evidence can travel with a member into another city or country?
- What would make the association trust GSN with its community memory?
- What would make the association fear losing control of its own story?

## 8. Meeting Format Upgrade

The meeting should now include a small **scenario card test**.

After the community problem section, show three short scenario cards:

1. Nigerian Society at RGU;
2. one institutional/community scenario, such as church, charity, or diaspora
   association;
3. one decision scenario, such as market trade or resident-to-merchant
   verification.

Ask participants:

- Which story feels most real?
- Which story best explains GSN?
- Which story feels forced?
- Which story creates the strongest need for evidence?
- Which story creates the greatest privacy or abuse risk?

This will reveal which market entry story participants understand without heavy
founder explanation.

### Facilitation guardrails

Use these rules throughout the meeting:

- Start with participant reality before showing the product.
- Do not defend GSN too quickly. Let objections breathe.
- Ask for examples, not opinions alone.
- Treat privacy concerns as product intelligence, not negativity.
- Separate "this is useful" from "we would actually use this".
- Separate "leaders like it" from "ordinary members would trust it".
- Separate "evidence helps" from "evidence proves everything".
- When participants say "trust", ask what behaviour, record, or person created
  that trust.
- When participants say "verification", ask who has the authority to confirm it
  and what could go wrong.
- When participants like every story, force a ranking.

The facilitator should keep returning to one question:

**What real decision becomes safer, fairer, or easier because this community
evidence exists?**

## 9. Workshop Hypotheses

The event should test these hypotheses:

1. Members recognise that their society already creates trust and community
   memory.
2. Members can name real moments where trust is needed but evidence is weak or
   scattered.
3. Members believe some community evidence should be preserved in structured
   form.
4. Members can distinguish membership, identity, endorsement, behaviour
   evidence, Trust Passport, and TrustSlip.
5. Members can identify who should be allowed to confirm different kinds of
   evidence.
6. Members can name what must stay private.
7. Members can name practical decisions GSN could support without guaranteeing
   the outcome.
8. Society leaders can imagine a governance process for confirming evidence,
   correcting mistakes, and preventing abuse.
9. Participants can identify which storyline best explains the value of GSN:
   student society, charity, market, church, diaspora association, or practical
   trade.
10. Participants can challenge whether each storyline preserves a real community
    process or merely sounds attractive.
11. Participants can identify at least one reason their society might reject or
    delay adoption.
12. Participants can describe the minimum version of GSN that would be useful
    before the full product vision is complete.

## 10. One-Hour Event Flow

### 0-5 minutes: Welcome

Set the tone:

**This is customer discovery, not a product launch. We are not asking you to
admire the app. We are asking you to challenge whether this solves a real
community problem.**

Facilitator prompt:

**Please be direct. If this feels unnecessary, risky, confusing, or too broad,
that is exactly the kind of feedback we need.**

### 5-12 minutes: Community problem

Ask before showing the product:

- When a new Nigerian student arrives at RGU, what do they need from the
  society?
- How does the society decide who is reliable, helpful, active, or safe to
  introduce?
- What useful community knowledge currently lives only in memory or WhatsApp?
- What gets lost when leaders graduate or members move away?
- What is one decision the society makes today with too little evidence?
- What would members be uncomfortable having recorded?

### 12-20 minutes: Trust map exercise

Draw a simple map with participants:

```text
Community
-> Membership
-> Activities
-> Support
-> Contributions
-> Introductions
-> Evidence
-> Trust Passport / TrustSlip
-> Safer decisions
```

Ask them to fill in real examples.

Keep the map grounded. For each example, ask:

- Who saw the behaviour?
- Who would be allowed to confirm it?
- Who should be allowed to see it later?
- What would make the record unfair or unsafe?

### 20-27 minutes: Scenario card test

Show three scenario cards from the storyline library.

For the RGU meeting, use:

1. Nigerian Society at RGU;
2. Pillar of Hope, church, or diaspora association;
3. Aberdeen resident trading with an unknown GSN merchant/member.

Ask which story feels most real, strongest, weakest, and riskiest.

Force a ranking:

```text
Most real:
Most useful:
Most risky:
Most confusing:
Best first pilot after RGU:
```

### 27-40 minutes: Focused app walkthrough

Show only the screens that support the community-first claim:

1. Community Home: where the society sees itself.
2. Member / Trust Passport: where member evidence gathers.
3. Trust Events or evidence categories: how behaviour becomes record.
4. TrustSlip: how selected evidence can support a decision.
5. Public verification: how an outsider checks current evidence and limits.

Do not tour every feature.

One sentence for the demo:

**This is how community activity becomes structured evidence, and how selected
evidence can travel safely without exposing everything.**

Demo rule:

If a screen needs a long explanation before the room understands why it matters,
skip it or mark it as weak. The workshop is testing clarity, not endurance.

### 40-53 minutes: Challenge discussion

Ask participants:

- What evidence would you trust from a society like this?
- Who should be allowed to confirm membership?
- Who should be allowed to confirm contribution, support, leadership, or
  reliability?
- What evidence would be dangerous or unfair to show publicly?
- What should a TrustSlip never claim?
- What would make this easy to abuse?
- What correction or appeal process would members expect?
- Which real society decisions could GSN support?
- Who would need to approve this before the society used it?
- What would the first useful version need to do, even if everything else came
  later?

### 53-60 minutes: Decision test and close

Give practical scenarios:

- A new student asks for housing help.
- A member wants to join a contribution group.
- A small vendor asks society members to buy or refer.
- Someone asks for emergency support.
- A member wants a job or placement referral.
- A leader is asked to confirm that someone belongs to the society.

For each scenario, ask:

- What evidence would help?
- Who should confirm it?
- What would still require human judgement?
- Would GSN be useful here, or would normal WhatsApp/referral be enough?

Close with four explicit decisions:

- Should the Nigerian Society continue as a pilot discovery partner?
- Which one use case should be tested first?
- Which evidence type is too risky for now?
- Who should be invited to the follow-up design session?

Close by asking who is willing to join a follow-up design session.

## 11. New Discovery Questions

### Pre-demo questions

- What does the Nigerian Society already help members do?
- Where does trust matter most in the society?
- How do members currently prove they belong?
- How do leaders currently know who is active, reliable, or supportive?
- What community knowledge gets lost over time?
- What problems happen because trust evidence is informal?
- Where would structured records help?
- Where would structured records be dangerous?
- Who currently carries too much trust knowledge in their head?
- What would be unfair to record about a member?
- What would be useful to remember but harmful to make public?

### Post-demo questions

- In your own words, what problem is GSN trying to solve?
- Did the app feel like community infrastructure or just a personal profile?
- Which evidence types felt useful?
- Which evidence types felt weak, unfair, or easy to fake?
- Who should control what the community records?
- Who should control what a member shares outside the community?
- What should be public, community-only, leader-only, or private?
- What decision would you actually use this for?
- What would stop the society from adopting something like this?
- Which storyline best explains GSN: student society, charity, market, church,
  diaspora association, or trade verification?
- Which storyline should be tested next with a real organisation?
- What is the smallest version of this that would still be useful?
- What would make you tell the society not to adopt it yet?
- What would make you personally comfortable or uncomfortable being recorded in
  it?

### Follow-up questions for weak answers

If participants give polite or vague answers, press gently:

- Can you give a real example?
- Who exactly would know that?
- Who would have the right to confirm it?
- Would ordinary members accept that, or only leaders?
- What could go wrong if this record followed someone outside the society?
- Is this something GSN should record, or should it stay in human judgement?

## 12. Evidence Ranking Exercise

Ask participants to rank these from strongest to weakest for society trust:

1. confirmed society membership;
2. active participation;
3. leadership or volunteer service;
4. contribution group history;
5. support received and repaid;
6. shop or merchant activity;
7. peer endorsement;
8. officer confirmation;
9. public TrustSlip verification;
10. phone or identity verification;
11. personal commitment history;
12. Trust band or score.

Then ask:

**What did we miss?**

The missing items may be more valuable than the list.

Then ask the harder question:

**Which of these would you trust only if you could see who confirmed it and when
it was last updated?**

This separates attractive labels from usable evidence.

## 13. What To Record Word For Word

Record exact participant language when they say:

- what the society already does for members;
- what trust means in their society;
- how they decide who to support or introduce;
- what evidence would make them more confident;
- what evidence would make them suspicious;
- what should never be public;
- what would make leaders adopt or reject GSN;
- which storyline made GSN clearest;
- which storyline felt exaggerated or not credible;
- what they would tell another student GSN does.

Do not clean the language too much. Discovery value is in the raw wording.

Also record:

- moments where participants disagree with each other;
- moments where leaders and ordinary members see the risk differently;
- moments where people say "this is useful, but...";
- words participants naturally use instead of GSN's formal language;
- any use case that creates visible energy in the room;
- any use case that makes the room go quiet.

The quiet moments matter. They often reveal privacy, status, fairness, or
authority risks before people can explain them neatly.

## 14. Pass / Concern / Fail Guide

### Pass

The session is a pass if most participants can:

- explain that GSN preserves community-created trust evidence;
- name real society activities that create trust;
- identify at least three evidence types worth recording;
- explain who should confirm different kinds of evidence;
- distinguish evidence from guarantee;
- name practical decisions GSN could support;
- identify at least one strong next segment to test, such as charity, market,
  church, diaspora association, or trade verification;
- challenge privacy, abuse, and fairness risks clearly;
- describe a first useful version that does not require the entire product to be
  complete.

### Concern

The session is a concern if participants:

- like the idea but still describe it as a personal reputation app;
- focus only on loans, savings, or marketplace;
- cannot say who owns evidence confirmation;
- trust the score/band more than the underlying evidence;
- feel the product path is too broad;
- worry that leaders could misuse evidence;
- like all stories politely but cannot say which one is strongest;
- cannot see why the society would adopt it;
- need heavy founder explanation before the community-first idea makes sense;
- see the product as promising safety, credit, or trust rather than supporting
  judgement with evidence.

### Fail

The session is a fail if most participants:

- cannot explain the community-first problem after the demo;
- do not believe their society creates trust worth preserving;
- reject structured evidence as unnecessary or unsafe;
- see no practical society decision GSN could support;
- find every scenario too abstract, exaggerated, or dependent on founder
  explanation;
- confuse GSN with a bank, credit bureau, or social media profile;
- leave more confused than when they arrived.

A fail is still useful if the reasons are captured honestly.

### Strongest possible learning

The best outcome is not universal praise. The best outcome is a clear answer to:

- which community problem is real;
- which evidence is worth preserving first;
- who has legitimate authority to confirm it;
- what must stay private;
- which use case creates adoption pull;
- which story should be dropped or rewritten.

## 15. Minimum Evidence Target

Leave the session with:

- at least 6 participant responses;
- at least 3 leader/organiser observations if leaders attend;
- top 5 trust-producing society activities;
- top 5 evidence types participants believe;
- top 5 privacy or abuse concerns;
- at least 5 direct quotes in participant language;
- at least 3 adoption blockers;
- a ranked list of the strongest next storylines to test;
- at least 3 privacy or misuse risks attached to the strongest storyline;
- a yes/no/maybe answer to whether Nigerian Society is a credible first
  community discovery partner;
- a named follow-up owner or organiser if the society wants another session;
- one recommended product walkthrough path for the next meeting.

---

## 16. Recommended Participants

Aim for 8-12 people:

- 2 current society leaders or committee members;
- 2 active society members;
- 2 newer students;
- 1 member involved in welfare/support/referrals if available;
- 1 member involved in business, trade, events, or contribution activity if
  available;
- 1 sceptic who is likely to challenge privacy, fairness, or usefulness.

Avoid filling the room only with friendly supporters.

If attendance is smaller, protect diversity over numbers. A five-person room
with one leader, two ordinary members, one newcomer, and one sceptic is more
valuable than twelve people who all share the same view.

## 17. Materials Needed

Prepare:

- one community-first slide;
- one trust map worksheet;
- three scenario cards from the storyline library;
- one focused app walkthrough;
- one feedback form;
- backup screenshots;
- one Trust Passport example;

The research is for method discipline, not for impressing participants.

Do not show every app surface. The walkthrough should feel like:

```text
community activity -> recorded evidence -> member evidence view -> selected
```

The walkthrough should take 10-13 minutes. Do not show the whole app.

Purpose:



### 2. Trust Events Or Evidence Categories

Purpose:

```text

```

```text
worth remembering, who can confirm them, and who should see them later.
```

Ask:

```text
Which society activities would be fair to record?
```

### 3. Trust Passport

Purpose:

```text
This is the member's gathered evidence view.
```

Say:

```text
This should help a member carry earned value, but it must not make them look
more verified than the evidence supports.
```

Ask:

```text
What would make this useful, and what would make it feel like a reputation
score?
```

### 4. TrustSlip

Purpose:

```text
This is selected evidence for a specific decision.
```

Say:

```text
A TrustSlip should help someone decide what to do next. It should not tell them
to release money, goods, housing, or trust automatically.
```

Ask:

```text
What should a TrustSlip never claim?
```

Purpose:

```text
This is what an outsider can check without seeing private community history.
```

Say:

```text
The public page must be useful enough to reduce uncertainty, but careful enough
not to expose private lives or pretend to guarantee safety.
```

Ask:


## 32. Note-Taking Rules

Use these rules for the person taking notes.

- Record participant words as closely as possible.
- Mark whether a comment came from a leader, active member, newer student, or
  sceptic, but avoid names unless follow-up permission is clear.
- Capture disagreement, not just consensus.
- Capture privacy discomfort even if people soften it.
- Capture examples of real decisions, not only opinions.
- Do not record private allegations about named people.
- If someone tells a sensitive story, write the pattern, not the identity.
- Mark founder explanations that were needed; those are clarity gaps.
- Mark any moment where the room misunderstands GSN as a bank, score, social
  media profile, or automatic verification system.
- Mark the exact phrases participants use instead of formal GSN language.

Use quick tags:

```text
[REAL] real use case
[RISK] privacy, misuse, unfairness, exposure
[AUTH] who can confirm
[BLOCKER] adoption barrier
[LANG] participant wording worth reusing
[CONFUSION] idea needed explanation
[PULL] participant asked how to use it or what comes next
```

Unabated truth: the most valuable note is often not the nicest quote. It is the
sentence that shows exactly why the product could fail.

- officer or volunteer role;

Why it may be strong:

## 35. Red Flags And Stop Rules

Use these as hard truth checks.

Stop or redesign the story if:

- participants mainly describe GSN as a personal reputation app;
- participants mainly describe it as a bank, loan app, credit bureau, or payment
  system;
- participants cannot name who should confirm evidence;
- ordinary members distrust leader-controlled records;
- privacy concerns are strong but vague because people are afraid to speak
  directly;
- participants like the idea but cannot name a decision where they would use it;
- the strongest use case depends on claims the current product cannot safely
  support;
- the demo requires long explanation before people understand the community
  problem;
- TrustSlip is interpreted as approval, guarantee, or instruction to release
  goods, money, housing, or trust;
- everyone is polite and nobody challenges anything.

Proceed only with a narrower pilot if:

- people see the community problem but disagree on evidence authority;
- leaders see value but ordinary members show discomfort;
- one use case is strong but the overall GSN story feels too broad;
- participants want GSN for internal memory but not public sharing yet;
- public verification feels useful but too risky for a first step.

Proceed to focused pilot design if:

- at least two participant types name the same real use case;
- participants can explain the value without founder language;
- privacy risks are named clearly enough to design around;
- authority rules are debatable but not invisible;
- a small group wants to test the next workflow.

Unabated truth: a smaller, narrower pilot is not a failure. It is usually the
first honest version of the product.

## 37. Facilitator Language Bank

Use this language when the room needs clarity. The point is to sound human,
specific, and honest without weakening the trust boundary.

### Plain GSN Explanation

```text
GSN is being tested as a way for communities to preserve useful evidence of the
trust, support, contribution, and responsibility they already create.
```

Shorter:

```text
GSN helps a community remember what matters and share only the evidence that is
appropriate for a decision.
```

Most human:

```text
Some people earn trust by showing up for their community. GSN is asking whether
that earned value can be remembered carefully and used responsibly.
```

### When Someone Calls It A Reputation App

Say:

```text
That is the danger we are trying to avoid. GSN should not reduce a person to a
popularity score. The question is whether specific community evidence can help a
specific decision.
```

Then ask:

```text
What evidence would be useful without turning the person into a score?
```

### When Someone Calls It A Bank Or Credit App

Say:

```text
GSN is not a bank and should not approve credit. It may help show evidence that
a community or person can consider before making a human decision.
```

Then ask:

```text
What would still need human judgement before any money, goods, or support moved?
```

### When Someone Says WhatsApp Is Enough

Say:

```text
That may be true for many things. I want to understand where WhatsApp and memory
are enough, and where the society loses something because nothing is preserved.
```

Then ask:

```text
What gets lost when leaders graduate, chats move on, or a member needs evidence
outside the group?
```

### When Leaders Like It But Members Are Quiet

Say:

```text
I want to hear the member view too. A system like this only works if ordinary
members feel it is fair and safe, not only useful for leaders.
```

Then ask:

```text
What would make this uncomfortable for an ordinary member?
```

### When Everyone Is Too Polite

Say:

```text
Let us make this harder. If you had to reject GSN, what would be the strongest
reason?
```

Then ask:

```text
Which part feels most risky, confusing, unnecessary, or easy to misuse?
```

### Words To Prefer

- community memory;
- useful evidence;
- human judgement;
- confirmed by the right person;
- private unless shared for a reason;
- helps a decision;
- does not guarantee;
- first useful version;
- what could go wrong.

### Words To Avoid In The Room

- behavioural capital;
- trust infrastructure instrument;
- score;
- automatic approval;
- creditworthiness;
- risk rating;
- surveillance;
- data extraction;
- user acquisition;
- monetisation;
- protocol, unless discussing formal internal research.

### Overclaims To Refuse

Do not say:

```text
GSN proves someone is trustworthy.
```

Say:

```text
GSN may show selected evidence that helps someone make a better judgement.
```

Do not say:

```text
GSN verifies a whole community.
```

Say:

```text
GSN may show recorded community evidence and the limits of what that evidence
does not prove.
```

Do not say:

```text
GSN can replace WhatsApp or leaders.
```

Say:

```text
GSN should preserve what the community needs to remember, while people still
make the human decisions.
```

Unabated truth: the best facilitator language makes GSN smaller, clearer, and
more trustworthy. If the explanation gets grander every time someone is
confused, the story is not ready.

## 38. Session Timebox Variants

Use the full one-hour plan when possible. If the room, venue, or participant
attention changes, do not improvise randomly. Switch to one of these versions.

### 30-Minute Fallback

Use this if people arrive late, the room is noisy, or the organiser only gives a
short slot.

```text
0-3 minutes: Welcome and consent
3-10 minutes: Community problem
10-17 minutes: Trust map
17-23 minutes: Show one app chain
23-28 minutes: Decision test
28-30 minutes: Follow-up ask
```

Must capture:

- one real community decision;
- one useful evidence type;
- one privacy risk;
- one authority question;
- whether the society is worth a deeper follow-up.

Skip:

- full scenario-card comparison;
- long app walkthrough;
- all secondary stories;
- detailed evidence ranking.

### 60-Minute Standard

Use the main workshop flow in section 10.

Protect these parts:

- community problem before product demo;
- trust map before app walkthrough;
- scenario ranking before final decision;
- privacy and authority questions before closing.

If time slips, cut demo time first. Do not cut participant examples.

### 90-Minute Deep Session

Use this if organisers offer more time and the group is engaged.

```text
0-8 minutes: Welcome, consent, and direct challenge invitation
8-20 minutes: Community problem
20-35 minutes: Trust map with examples
35-50 minutes: Scenario cards and ranking
50-65 minutes: Focused app walkthrough
65-80 minutes: Evidence ranking and authority mapping
80-88 minutes: Pilot use-case decision
88-90 minutes: Follow-up group ask
```

Extra depth to add:

- split leaders and ordinary members for five minutes if leaders dominate;
- ask each group to name one evidence type they would record and one they would
  refuse to record;
- compare answers before closing.

### Timebox Rule

If forced to choose, protect learning over presentation.

```text
More participant examples, less demo.
More objections, less persuasion.
More authority/privacy clarity, less feature explanation.
```

## 39. Room Roles

Assign roles before the session starts. One person can hold more than one role
only if the group is small.

### Facilitator

Job:

- keep the room honest;
- ask the first question before showing product;
- stop overexplaining;
- invite quiet participants in;
- force ranking when feedback becomes polite.

Must not:

- defend every objection;
- turn the session into a feature tour;
- let leaders answer for everyone;
- convert interest into validation during the meeting.

### Note Taker

Job:

- capture exact participant language;
- tag notes with `[REAL]`, `[RISK]`, `[AUTH]`, `[BLOCKER]`, `[LANG]`,
  `[CONFUSION]`, and `[PULL]`;
- record disagreements;
- protect privacy by avoiding named sensitive stories.

Must not:

- clean up language too much;
- write only positive quotes;
- miss who disagreed with whom.

### Timekeeper

Job:

- protect the workshop shape;
- warn at halfway and ten minutes remaining;
- cut the demo if the discussion is producing useful evidence;
- make sure the session reaches a decision test.

Must not:

- let the app walkthrough consume the room;
- let one participant dominate the whole session.

### Observer

Optional but useful.

Job:

- watch body language and quiet moments;
- notice when members become cautious;
- note when leaders and members react differently;
- flag moments where the product sounds too broad.

Must not:

- interrupt the facilitator constantly;
- become a second presenter.

### Role Assignment Template

```text
Facilitator:
Note taker:
Timekeeper:
Observer:
Backup app/screenshots owner:
Follow-up owner:
```

Unabated truth: if one person facilitates, demos, takes notes, watches time, and
interprets the room, important evidence will be missed. Discovery needs
attention, not heroics.

## 40. Follow-Up Design Session Plan

Use this only if the first discovery session produces enough signal to continue.
The follow-up should not repeat the broad discovery conversation. It should turn
one use case into a testable pilot workflow.

### Purpose

```text
Choose the first useful GSN workflow for the Nigerian Society and define what
evidence, authority, privacy, and member benefit must look like before a pilot.
```

### Who To Invite

Aim for 5-7 people:

- 1-2 society leaders or committee members;
- 2 ordinary active members;
- 1 newer student;
- 1 privacy/fairness sceptic;
- 1 person connected to the selected use case, such as welfare, events,
  contribution groups, referrals, or trade.

Do not invite only people who liked the first session.

### Ninety-Minute Design Flow

```text
0-10 minutes: Recap what the first session proved and did not prove
10-25 minutes: Choose one use case only
25-40 minutes: Map the real-world workflow before GSN
40-55 minutes: Decide what evidence belongs in the workflow
55-70 minutes: Decide who can confirm, correct, hide, or share evidence
70-82 minutes: Review the GSN screen chain needed for the pilot
82-90 minutes: Decide pilot scope, owner, and next action
```

### Design Questions

- What is the real decision this workflow supports?
- What does the society already do without GSN?
- What evidence is useful enough to record?
- What evidence should stay private or out of scope?
- Who can confirm the evidence?
- Who can challenge or correct it?
- What does the member get from participating?
- What would make the workflow feel unfair?
- What is the smallest version worth testing?

### Output

Leave with:

- one pilot use case;
- one primary user journey;
- one evidence list;
- one privacy boundary;
- one authority rule;
- one correction rule;
- one follow-up owner;
- one test date or decision deadline.

Devil's advocate: if the follow-up design session still wants to discuss every
GSN feature, the first discovery session did not narrow the problem enough.

## 42. Product Translation Rules

Use these rules after discovery. Participant feedback should shape the product,
but it should not automatically become a feature request.

### Translate Problems Before Features

If a participant says:

```text
We need a button for leaders to approve people.
```

Translate first:

```text
The community needs a controlled way for recognised authority to confirm a
membership or evidence event.
```

Then decide whether the product needs:

- a button;
- a workflow;
- a policy rule;
- a notification;
- a private note;
- a public evidence boundary;
- no product change yet.

### Translate Excitement Into Evidence

If participants are excited, ask what the excitement proves.

Good evidence:

- they name a real decision;
- they name who would use it;
- they name what would be recorded;
- they name what must stay private;
- they ask for a follow-up workflow test.

Weak evidence:

- they say the app is nice;
- they say it could be big;
- they like every story;
- they ask for many unrelated features;
- they cannot name what they would use first.

### Translate Objections Into Design Constraints

Do not treat objections as resistance to overcome. Treat them as design
material.

```text
Privacy objection -> visibility rule.
Leader misuse concern -> authority and audit rule.
Score concern -> evidence-over-score copy and UI boundary.
WhatsApp-is-enough concern -> preserve only what WhatsApp loses.
Bank/credit confusion -> remove finance overclaiming from the story.
```

### Translate Silence Into Risk

Silence can mean consent, confusion, discomfort, politeness, or fear of
contradicting leaders. Do not assume it means agreement.

If ordinary members are quiet, record:

```text
Member-side signal is weak. Do not claim community adoption until ordinary
members describe value and risk in their own words.
```

### Translate One Community Carefully

Do not generalise too quickly.

```text
Nigerian Society signal may support student-society discovery.
It does not automatically validate churches, markets, charities, diaspora
associations, finance workflows, merchant release, or public TrustSlip usage.
```

Use the RGU session to choose the next segment, not to claim the whole market.

### Translation Output

Every product decision after the session should be written as:

```text
Participant evidence:

Problem:

Design constraint:

Smallest testable product response:

What we are not building yet:

Risk if we overbuild:
```

Unabated truth: discovery does not give permission to build everything people
mention. It gives permission to test the smallest real workflow that keeps
showing up under pressure.

## 43. Learning Backlog Template

Use this backlog after the session. It separates evidence from interpretation
and stops weak signals from becoming roadmap commitments.

```text
Learning item:

Source:
[ ] participant quote
[ ] repeated pattern
[ ] disagreement
[ ] silence / discomfort
[ ] facilitator observation
[ ] feedback form

Participant type:
[ ] leader / committee
[ ] active member
[ ] newer student
[ ] sceptic
[ ] other:

Raw evidence:

What this may mean:

Confidence:
[ ] low
[ ] medium
[ ] high

Product implication:
[ ] copy/language
[ ] workflow
[ ] permission/authority
[ ] privacy/visibility
[ ] evidence model
[ ] TrustSlip boundary
[ ] onboarding
[ ] not a product change yet

Smallest next test:

Do not build yet:

Owner:

Review date:
```

### Backlog Priority

Prioritise learning items in this order:

1. Risks that could harm members or expose private information.
2. Confusions that make GSN look like a score, bank, social app, or guarantee.
3. Repeated real decisions participants want evidence for.
4. Authority questions that must be settled before a pilot.
5. Language participants use more naturally than GSN's internal terms.
6. Feature ideas only after the above are clear.

### Kill Criteria

Move an idea out of the active backlog if:

- it came from only one polite suggestion;
- no real decision depends on it;
- it requires backend or governance complexity before the use case is proven;
- it makes GSN look like a bank, credit bureau, surveillance tool, or public
  reputation score;
- it weakens privacy or authority boundaries;
- it belongs to a later segment, not the RGU pilot.

Devil's advocate: a backlog full of interesting ideas can still be a weak
backlog. The strongest backlog protects the first narrow test from distraction.

The plan does not prove:

- willingness to pay;

  memory story;

## 46.1 Pilot Entry Recovery Rule

Add this to the pilot operating checklist before inviting real members through
the join line.

```text
Approval -> activation -> phone check -> private recovery -> community growth
```

Rationale:

- A member who reaches forgot-password without private recovery is already in a
  support dead end.
- Recovery setup should be surfaced while the person is entering or activating
  a GSN identity, not after they are locked out.
- The join request itself should stay light. Do not ask unapproved applicants to
  create recovery prompts before the community has accepted them.
- After approval, activation should explain that password, phone check, and
  private recovery are part of safe account setup.
- If private recovery is missing later, the forgot-password page must send the
  member to owner / GSN support review instead of pretending automatic recovery
  can continue.

Devil's advocate:

```text
This protects future members, but it does not rescue existing accounts that
already skipped recovery. Those accounts still need owner/support review.
```

Smallest product response:

- keep the existing private recovery setup on Identity & Integrity;
- route activated members with verified phones to the Recovery task before First
  Circle growth;
- keep phone verification first when the phone is not verified;
- for owner / GSN support review, use admin Identity Risk phone-lineage lookup
  to confirm the protected phone identity and private recovery readiness;
- record recovery setup as a standard pilot procedure, not an optional hidden
  profile chore.
