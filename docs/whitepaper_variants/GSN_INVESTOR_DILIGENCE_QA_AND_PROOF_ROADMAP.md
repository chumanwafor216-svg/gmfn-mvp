# GSN Investor Diligence Q&A And Proof Roadmap

Last updated: 2026-07-09

Use this before investor conversations. It is not a pitch deck. It is a discipline sheet for answering hard questions without overclaiming.

## Investor Position

GSN is an early Trust Infrastructure thesis. It is not yet a product-market-fit claim.

The honest investor framing is:

```text
GSN is testing whether communities will preserve and permission community-recognised trust evidence for real decisions in membership, commerce, support, verification, and community finance.
```

## What Is Strong Today

- The problem is intuitive: communities already know who contributes, keeps promises, helps, leads, trades honestly, and can be trusted with responsibility.
- The current systems of memory, WhatsApp, receipts, introductions, and leader judgement are useful but fragile.
- GSN has a clear infrastructure thesis: preserve provenance-rich community evidence without reducing people to a public score.
- The first customer discovery path is concrete: RGU/Nigerian Society or another real community context.
- The language now recognises major risks: privacy, consent, correction, misuse, authority, governance, and social-scoring fear.

## What Is Not Proven Yet

- Which first wedge will pull hardest.
- Whether communities will consistently record evidence.
- Whether members will consent to share selected evidence.
- Whether external decision-makers will trust the evidence enough to use it.
- Whether willingness to pay exists, and from whom.
- Whether the governance model survives conflict, bias, correction requests, and authority disputes.
- Whether GSN can avoid being perceived as reputation scoring.

## Founder Brief Gap Discipline

Before rewriting or sending the founder/investor brief, complete:

```text
GSN_FOUNDERS_BRIEF_GAP_CLOSURE.md
```

The brief must answer these questions plainly. If the answer is not yet proven,
say so instead of turning the vision into a claim.

| Question | Required discipline |
| --- | --- |
| What services may generate revenue? | Name services, but separate possible revenue from proven revenue. |
| Who exactly pays first? | Name the likely first payer or sponsor; do not assume ordinary members pay first. |
| What is the first use case? | Pick one narrow decision, not "all community trust." |
| What is the first pilot? | Name one community, one organiser, one workflow, and one evidence output before calling it pilot-ready. |
| What has already been built? | Distinguish source-level MVP surfaces from accepted pilot evidence. |
| What traction exists? | Do not claim traction unless interviews, pilot usage, LOIs, payment, or repeated use exist. |
| How is information verified? | Explain provenance, confirmation, timestamp, community context, and sharing purpose. |
| How are consent and privacy protected? | State private-by-default, purpose-bound sharing, and no raw private details on public surfaces. |
| How can somebody challenge inaccurate evidence? | Describe dispute, context, admin review, contested status, and correction history. |
| How does information expire or become irrelevant? | Show currentness, expiry/review dates, replacement, withdrawal, revoked, or stale states. |
| How will GSN avoid becoming social credit? | Refuse global scores, public rankings, and permanent numerical human grades. |
| What exact partnership or investment is requested? | Ask for a pilot partner, sponsor, technical/product review partner, or milestone-tied capital. |

## The Best Early Investor Ask

```text
I am not asking you to treat this as product-market fit. I am asking whether this is a venture-scale infrastructure problem, and what evidence you would need from customer discovery and a narrow pilot before the investment case becomes serious.
```

## Hard Questions And Honest Answers

### 1. Is this just a reputation app?

No, but this is the biggest perception risk.

GSN should not rank people by popularity or produce a global score. The thesis is that specific community evidence can support specific human decisions when provenance, consent, correction, and context remain visible.

Proof needed:

- participants can explain GSN as evidence infrastructure, not personal scoring;
- users can name what should never be public;
- first pilot uses decision-specific evidence, not a global reputation number.

### 2. Why would a community adopt this?

A community may adopt if it already loses value because trust evidence is scattered or forgotten.

Possible adoption triggers:

- leaders changing;
- members graduating or relocating;
- support decisions needing more accountability;
- trusted trade or referrals needing verification;
- community contribution needing continuity;
- external partners asking for evidence.

Proof needed:

- one community names a painful decision where better evidence matters;
- an organiser agrees to test a narrow workflow;
- members see value without feeling exposed.

### 3. Who pays?

This is not proven.

Possible payers:

- community organisations that need structured membership/trust records;
- merchants or members who need trusted verification;
- diaspora/community groups coordinating support and opportunity;
- institutions or partners that need safer evidence channels;
- later, premium TrustSlip/verification or community administration layers.

Proof needed:

- willingness-to-pay interviews;
- a first paid pilot or strong budget-holder signal;
- evidence that the buyer is not always different from the daily user.

### 4. What is the wedge?

The safest current wedge is not "all communities." That is too broad.

Candidate wedges:

1. Student/diaspora community trust and introductions.
2. Community merchant/member verification.
3. Community support and contribution evidence.
4. Association/church/charity service and responsibility records.

The first discovery sessions should decide which one has the strongest pull.

Proof needed:

- one wedge produces sharper pain, faster understanding, and clearer next action than the others;
- participants can describe the use case without founder explanation.

### 5. What is the moat?

The possible moat is not a feature list. It is the trusted evidence graph and governance layer around community-recognised behaviour.

Potential defensibility:

- provenance-rich trust records;
- community authority and confirmation workflows;
- permissioned Trust Passport/TrustSlip outputs;
- accumulated behavioural continuity across contexts;
- governance and correction mechanisms that make the evidence credible.

Proof needed:

- communities create records repeatedly;
- external viewers trust the evidence format;
- participants care about provenance and correction, not only the interface.

### 6. How do you avoid social-credit risk?

By refusing global scoring logic and keeping evidence tied to:

- a specific community;
- a specific behaviour or event;
- a specific confirmation source;
- a specific sharing purpose;
- consent and correction routes;
- human judgement at the decision point.

Proof needed:

- no first pilot depends on public ranking;
- participants can challenge records;
- sensitive evidence stays private by default;
- the product language avoids "score proves trust."

### 7. Is this regulated finance?

GSN should not claim to approve credit, guarantee repayment, hold funds, or automate financial decisions.

It may support human decisions by making relevant community evidence clearer. If later finance features create regulated obligations, those rails need separate legal, compliance, and operational design.

Proof needed:

- finance-facing language remains evidence/support only;
- no investor or pilot material implies automated credit approval;
- legal/compliance review before regulated claims.

### 8. Why now?

The likely "why now" is that communities already coordinate through digital tools, but those tools are fragmented and poor at preserving trusted context.

Possible timing forces:

- diaspora mobility;
- informal community commerce;
- remote trust decisions;
- fragmented WhatsApp/social records;
- rising need for verifiable but privacy-aware community evidence;
- institutions seeking safer non-traditional evidence.

Proof needed:

- participants describe the fragmentation problem without being led;
- external decision-makers confirm the current evidence channels are insufficient.

### 9. What would make this fail?

- Communities do not care enough to record evidence.
- Members fear surveillance or unfair judgement.
- Leaders abuse confirmation authority.
- The product feels like social scoring.
- The first wedge is too broad.
- The buyer is unclear.
- External viewers do not trust the evidence.
- Governance and correction are too weak.

## Proof Roadmap

### Stage 1: Discovery Proof

Goal:

```text
Show that real communities can name trust decisions, evidence sources, refusal points, and a first use case.
```

Evidence to collect:

- 10-20 customer discovery interviews;
- one organiser willing to host a structured session;
- ranked use cases;
- exact participant language;
- clear refusal reasons;
- first wedge recommendation.

Pass signal:

- one use case is repeatedly named without prompting;
- users describe current evidence pain clearly;
- privacy and authority risks are specific, not vague.

### Stage 2: Pilot Proof

Goal:

```text
Show that one community will record and share selected evidence for one real decision.
```

Evidence to collect:

- one pilot community;
- one defined workflow;
- records created by real users;
- member consent behaviour;
- correction/challenge handling;
- at least one external or semi-external verification moment.

Pass signal:

- participants use the workflow more than once;
- the organiser sees reduced confusion or stronger accountability;
- members trust the sharing boundary.

### Stage 3: Commercial Proof

Goal:

```text
Show who pays, why they pay, and whether the value repeats.
```

Evidence to collect:

- budget-holder interviews;
- pricing tests;
- pilot conversion or paid letter of intent;
- retention or repeated use;
- evidence that governance does not kill adoption.

Pass signal:

- a buyer accepts a narrow paid use case;
- usage continues after founder attention reduces;
- the buyer can explain value in their own words.

## Investor Conversation Rules

- Lead with the problem and proof plan, not the grand vision.
- Say "thesis" until discovery and pilot evidence justify stronger language.
- Do not claim product-market fit.
- Do not hide the social-scoring objection.
- Do not say GSN is a bank, lender, credit scorer, or risk-free verifier.
- Do not pitch all communities at once.
- Do not confuse app completeness with market proof.

## Best Investor Close

```text
The honest next step is not a large investment claim. It is a sharper proof path: discovery, one narrow pilot, and evidence of repeatable value. What would you need to see from those steps before this becomes an investable infrastructure thesis?
```

## Devil's Advocate

Investors will not fund poetry for long. The story is interesting, but the investable case depends on wedge clarity, buyer clarity, repeated behaviour, governance credibility, and evidence that GSN is not perceived as social scoring.

If customer discovery cannot produce one painful first decision and one credible buyer/user path, the investor brief should stay in the drawer.
