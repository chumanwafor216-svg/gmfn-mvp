# GSN White Paper Variant Send Guide

Last updated: 2026-07-09

This guide separates the three white paper copies by audience. Do not send the same copy to everyone. Each version has a different job.

## 1. Chris Mulley / RGU Academic Copy

File:

`GSN_Trust_Infrastructure_RGU_Academic_Copy_Chris_Mulley.docx`

Audience:

Chris Mulley, Director of Innovation and Entrepreneurship, Robert Gordon University.

Purpose:

Academic and innovation evaluation. This version should invite scrutiny, not sound like a sales pitch.

Best use:

Send before or after a meeting where you want RGU to understand the theory, research opportunity, customer discovery plan, and possible pathway toward structured evaluation.

Core message:

GSN is not asking RGU to accept a finished product. It is asking RGU to help examine whether the Trust Infrastructure framework describes a real societal phenomenon and whether a responsible pilot should be designed.

Suggested subject line:

`GSN Trust Infrastructure - Academic Evaluation Copy for Customer Discovery`

Suggested email:

```text
Dear Chris,

Thank you again for the guidance and challenge around GSN.

I have prepared a shorter academic evaluation copy of the Trust Infrastructure paper for your review. This version is written specifically for RGU and frames GSN as a research and customer discovery opportunity rather than as a finished product.

The main question I am asking is whether the framework describes a real societal phenomenon: communities already recognise behavioural consistency, but much of that recognised trust is lost when people move between communities, institutions, and opportunities.

I would be grateful for your view on whether this is worth taking into a focused customer discovery process, and what evidence RGU would need before considering deeper evaluation or support.

Kind regards,
Nwafor Chuma
```

What not to say:

- Do not say RGU has endorsed GSN.
- Do not imply the theory is already proven.
- Do not lead with visa pressure inside the paper. If needed, mention timing politely in the email or meeting conversation.

## 2. Customer Discovery Plain-Language Copy

File:

`GSN_Customer_Discovery_Plain_Language_Copy.docx`

Audience:

Community members, society leaders, students, organisers, market/community participants, and early pilot groups.

Purpose:

Help normal people understand the problem and challenge the idea. This version should be simple, human, and testable.

Best use:

Use before or during discovery sessions. It should support conversation, not replace conversation.

Core message:

Every community already knows who shows up, contributes, keeps promises, helps, leads, repays, sells honestly, or can be trusted with responsibility. GSN asks whether that useful community evidence can be preserved responsibly so people do not keep starting from zero.

Suggested subject line:

`Plain GSN Discovery Brief - For Your Honest Feedback`

Suggested message:

```text
Hi,

I am sharing a plain-language GSN discovery brief before our conversation.

The aim is not to sell you a finished app. I want to test whether the problem is real in your community: useful trust, contribution, responsibility, and participation records often live in memory, WhatsApp chats, leaders' heads, receipts, or old committee knowledge.

Please read it with honesty. The most useful feedback is not praise. It is whether the idea feels real, risky, confusing, unnecessary, or useful for one specific decision in your community.

Thank you,
Chuma
```

What not to say:

- Do not call it a reputation app.
- Do not call it a scoring system.
- Do not over-explain Behavioural Capital in the first conversation.
- Do not ask people if they like the platform before asking whether the problem is real.

Session opener:

```text
Before we talk about GSN as an app, I want to ask about your community. How do people currently know who is reliable, who contributes, who can be trusted with responsibility, or who needs more checking before a decision is made?
```

## 3. Investor Brief

File:

`GSN_Trust_Infrastructure_Investor_Brief.docx`

Audience:

Angel investors, early-stage funds, innovation funders, strategic partners, and venture advisors.

Purpose:

Explain the venture thesis without pretending adoption is already proven.

Best use:

Use only when the reader is evaluating GSN as a potential venture, infrastructure opportunity, or early investment conversation.

Core message:

GSN is building Trust Infrastructure: a community-first system for preserving provenance-rich behavioural evidence across membership, commerce, contribution, support, and institutional decision-making.

Suggested subject line:

`GSN Trust Infrastructure - Early Investor Brief`

Suggested message:

```text
Hi,

I am sharing an early investor brief for GSN.

GSN is not another social network or marketplace. The thesis is that communities already create valuable behavioural evidence through contribution, responsibility, trade, support, leadership, and participation, but that evidence is usually fragmented and lost across transitions.

This brief explains the infrastructure opportunity, the first wedge markets, the business model hypotheses, and the risks that still need validation through customer discovery and pilots.

I would value your honest view on whether this is a venture-scale infrastructure problem and what proof you would need to see before taking the opportunity further.

Kind regards,
Nwafor Chuma
```

What not to say:

- Do not claim investor readiness as if product-market fit is proven.
- Do not pitch all communities at once.
- Do not hide the social-credit objection.
- Do not present Behavioural Capital as an accepted market category yet. Present it as a testable thesis.

Investor proof still needed:

- Clear first wedge.
- Discovery evidence from real communities.
- Pilot usage and retention.
- Willingness to pay.
- Governance and privacy validation.
- A sharper answer to how GSN avoids becoming reputation scoring.

## Recommended Send Order

1. Send Chris/RGU academic copy first if the immediate goal is customer discovery under RGU guidance.
2. Use the customer discovery copy in the room with participants after RGU or an organiser helps open the door.
3. Use the investor copy only after you can say what the first pilot wedge is and what evidence you are collecting.

## One Sentence For All Audiences

```text
GSN does not create trust; it helps communities preserve and communicate the trust evidence they already recognise, with provenance, consent, and human judgement intact.
```

## UN SDG Wording For All Audiences

If asked how GSN relates to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals,
use careful development-impact language:

```text
GSN aligns with selected UN Sustainable Development Goals by helping communities
preserve identity, participation, trust evidence, commerce, governance, and
opportunity records. It should not be described as fulfilling those goals until
real deployments produce measured outcomes.
```

Strongest alignment to mention:

- SDG 16: Peace, Justice and Strong Institutions
- SDG 8: Decent Work and Economic Growth
- SDG 1: No Poverty
- SDG 9: Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure
- SDG 10: Reduced Inequalities
- SDG 11: Sustainable Cities and Communities
- SDG 17: Partnerships for the Goals

Only mention SDG 4, SDG 5, SDG 12, or SDG 13 when the conversation is about a
specific education, gender inclusion, responsible local production, or
climate/community resilience use case.

## Unabated Truth

The academic and customer discovery copies are the priority right now. The investor copy is useful, but it becomes much stronger after one real discovery session produces evidence about the first pilot use case.
