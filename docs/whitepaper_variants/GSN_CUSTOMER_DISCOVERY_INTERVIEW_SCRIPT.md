# GSN Customer Discovery Interview Script

Last updated: 2026-07-09

Use this with the plain-language customer discovery copy. It is for one-to-one interviews or small groups of 2-4 people. For a full room workshop, use the RGU community discovery session pack.

## Purpose

Find out whether GSN is solving a real community problem, not whether people like the idea.

The interview should produce evidence about:

- where community trust currently lives;
- which decisions need better evidence;
- what people would refuse to record or share;
- who has authority to confirm evidence;
- whether the first pilot use case is obvious enough.

## Opening Script

```text
Thank you for speaking with me.

This is not a sales conversation and I am not asking you to agree with the idea. I am trying to find out whether GSN solves a real problem or whether I am forcing a founder story onto people.

Please be direct. The most useful feedback is where the idea feels risky, confusing, unnecessary, unfair, or too broad.

Do not share private names or sensitive details about another person. If you need to describe an example, describe the pattern.
```

## The Core Question

```text
In your community, how do people currently know who is reliable, who contributes, who can be trusted with responsibility, or who needs more checking before a decision is made?
```

Do not explain GSN before asking this. Let the participant describe the world as it is.

## 30-Minute Interview Flow

```text
0-3 minutes: Consent, purpose, and honesty request
3-10 minutes: Current community trust process
10-17 minutes: Real decision where evidence matters
17-23 minutes: Show the plain GSN explanation
23-28 minutes: Risks, refusal points, and safeguards
28-30 minutes: Close with first-pilot decision
```

If time is short, skip the app explanation and keep the current-process questions.

## 45-Minute Interview Flow

```text
0-5 minutes: Consent, purpose, and honesty request
5-15 minutes: Current community trust process
15-25 minutes: Evidence map
25-32 minutes: Plain GSN explanation and reaction
32-40 minutes: Safeguards, privacy, authority, and misuse
40-45 minutes: Pilot ranking and close
```

## Questions To Ask

### 1. Current Reality

Ask:

```text
When someone is new, unknown, or being considered for responsibility, how does the community currently decide whether to trust them?
```

Follow-ups:

- Who gets asked?
- What evidence matters?
- What is written down?
- What lives only in memory or messages?
- What gets lost when leaders change or members leave?

### 2. Decisions

Ask:

```text
What is one real decision where better community evidence would help?
```

Possible prompts:

- welcoming a new member;
- confirming membership;
- choosing someone for responsibility;
- deciding who can represent the group;
- buying from or referring a member;
- emergency support;
- contribution or repayment trust;
- housing, jobs, placement, or local advice.

Force one answer:

```text
If GSN could support only one decision first, which decision should it be?
```

### 3. Evidence

Ask:

```text
What kind of behaviour would be useful to record carefully?
```

Probe:

- attendance;
- contribution;
- service;
- leadership;
- repayment;
- trade honesty;
- support given;
- support received;
- dispute resolution;
- introductions or referrals.

Then ask:

```text
Which of these should never be public?
```

### 4. Authority

Ask:

```text
Who should be allowed to confirm that something happened?
```

Follow-ups:

- Should only officers confirm?
- Can peers confirm?
- Can merchants or external people confirm?
- What happens if confirmation is wrong?
- Who can correct or challenge a record?

### 5. Trust And Refusal

Ask:

```text
What would make you refuse to use GSN?
```

Do not soften the question. Wait for the real answer.

Probe:

- privacy;
- gossip;
- bias;
- unfair punishment;
- public shame;
- leaders abusing power;
- wrong records;
- pressure to share;
- people becoming reduced to a score.

### 6. Plain GSN Reaction

After current-reality questions, say:

```text
GSN does not create trust. It tries to help a community preserve and share the trust evidence it already recognises, with consent, correction, provenance, and human judgement.
```

Then ask:

```text
Does that match the problem you described, or am I solving the wrong thing?
```

## Signal Scoring

Strong signal:

- The participant names a real decision before you suggest one.
- They explain where evidence currently lives.
- They argue about who should confirm records.
- They identify a specific privacy or misuse risk.
- They can name a first pilot use case.
- They ask how soon the community could test it.

Medium signal:

- They understand the idea but need examples.
- They agree the problem exists but struggle to name the first decision.
- They like the trust idea but worry about governance.

Weak signal:

- They mainly say "it is interesting."
- They only talk about app features.
- They cannot name a real decision.
- They do not care who confirms evidence.
- They see no privacy or misuse risk.

Fail signal:

- They read it as social scoring.
- They think WhatsApp/memory already solves the problem well enough.
- They cannot see why a community would record anything.
- They think the risk of misuse is larger than the benefit.

## Close The Interview

Ask:

```text
If I do nothing else, what is the one thing I should fix before showing this to another person?
```

Then:

```text
Would you be willing to join a follow-up session where we design the first safe pilot workflow?
```

Record:

```text
[ ] yes
[ ] maybe
[ ] no
Contact:
Condition:
```

## What Not To Say

- Do not ask, "Do you like GSN?"
- Do not ask, "Would you use this app?" before you understand the decision.
- Do not say GSN will make people trustworthy.
- Do not say GSN will approve loans, trades, jobs, or membership.
- Do not promise the privacy model is solved.
- Do not hide the score/social-credit concern.
- Do not argue with the participant if they reject the idea.

## Devil's Advocate

If the participant cannot describe a real trust decision, a real evidence source, and a real risk, the interview has not yet produced enough learning.

Praise is not evidence. A clear refusal reason is more valuable than a vague compliment.
