# GSN RGU Meeting Brief For Chris Mulley

Last updated: 2026-07-09

Use this for the conversation with Chris Mulley. The goal is to get academic/customer-discovery guidance, not to make RGU bless the product.

## Meeting Objective

Leave the meeting with one of three clear outcomes:

1. Chris agrees the problem is worth structured customer discovery.
2. Chris says the idea is not yet clear enough and names what must be sharpened.
3. Chris points to the right RGU route, person, process, or evidence standard for the next step.

Anything softer than that is polite encouragement, not progress.

## Opening Position

```text
Chris, I am not asking RGU to endorse GSN as a proven product. I am asking whether the Trust Infrastructure thesis is strong enough to take into structured customer discovery, and what evidence would make the next step academically responsible.
```

## Two-Minute Explanation

GSN starts from a simple observation: communities already recognise trust, contribution, reliability, responsibility, and participation, but that evidence often stays trapped in memory, informal messages, receipts, committee knowledge, or leader judgement.

When people move between communities, institutions, markets, and opportunities, they often start from zero again, even when a real history of responsible behaviour exists somewhere.

GSN asks whether that community-recognised evidence can be preserved responsibly, with provenance, consent, correction rights, and human judgement, so it can support better decisions without becoming a score-based reputation system.

## What To Ask Chris

1. Does this read as a real research/customer-discovery problem, or only as founder logic?
2. Which part is strongest: community trust evidence, behavioural continuity, diaspora/community mobility, marketplace trust, lending/support, or institutional verification?
3. Which part is weakest or most dangerous?
4. What would RGU need to see before treating this as a credible innovation/customer discovery project?
5. Is the RGU Nigerian Society or another student/community group an appropriate first discovery context?
6. Who else inside or around RGU should review this before a pilot is discussed?
7. What evidence should be collected in the first discovery session so the result is useful academically, not just encouraging?

## What To Listen For

Strong signal:

- Chris challenges the wording but accepts the problem is worth testing.
- He asks about governance, privacy, consent, correction, misuse, or ethics.
- He suggests a specific discovery route, reviewer, department, or pilot boundary.
- He helps narrow the first use case.

Weak signal:

- He says it sounds interesting but gives no next step.
- He treats it only as an app idea.
- He avoids the trust/governance risk.
- He does not identify who should be involved next.

Red signal:

- He thinks the concept sounds like social scoring.
- He thinks the academic language is too abstract for discovery.
- He says the proposed first community is not appropriate.
- He cannot see a clear research question or customer segment.

## Do Not Do In The Meeting

- Do not ask for endorsement.
- Do not pitch investors.
- Do not lead with visa pressure.
- Do not talk as if Behavioural Capital is already an accepted category.
- Do not claim GSN has solved privacy or governance.
- Do not call it a scoring system, reputation app, or social-credit alternative.
- Do not show every scenario at once.

## The Best Ask

```text
Would you be willing to advise whether this should proceed into a structured customer discovery session, and if yes, what safeguards and evidence questions should be built into that session from the beginning?
```

## Devil's Advocate

The biggest risk is that the meeting becomes flattering but useless. A good meeting should make the idea sharper, narrower, and more testable. If Chris does not identify a next route, a first community, a research weakness, or a stronger evidence requirement, then the meeting has not yet moved the work forward.
